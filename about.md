I'm a systems engineer by trade, a molecular biologist by education and a decidedly amateur polymath. Online personality tests tell me I'm an INTP and an enneagram 5w4 if you put stock into that sort of thing - though a buzzfeed quiz I took says that if I was a cake, I would be tiramasu, which may be just as relevant.

After a short stint as a software engineering intern in high school, I decided "tech" wasn't for me, and decided I wanted to be a scientist. I liked to code, but I couldn't see myself doing that for the rest of my life.

In college I was interested in the metagenomics of microbiomes, and how communities of microbes influenced our health and the health of our surroundings. I ended up as a research assistant in an immunology lab, where I dissected mice, used very expensive scientific instruments, and generally annoyed grad students. One day I let it slip that I knew how to script, and the PI of the lab encouraged me to apply for a "computional immunology internship," where I took genomic data sequenced from those mice, stitched together a few tools other people had made, wrote some stuff in python and r, threw it on our campus supercomputer and ta-da! Science. 

That got me an authorship on a scientific paper published in the Journal of Immunology. Pretty cool. It'd look great on my PhD applications. 

To pay bills in college I got a job at a small break/fix MSP in town that was severely understaffed - the pay was low, there was a lot to learn, but the other techs were chill and it was work I enjoyed learning about and doing.

As college slogged on I realized a few things. A) I really didn't want to spend another 6 - 8 years in grad school, B) If I didn't go to grad school, a degree in biology would be pretty much useless and C) I was enjoying my time doing IT far more than I was my time in the lab, and D) I found infrastructure far more interesting than scripting for data science. 

So long story short, I'm a systems engineer instead of a PhD student and probably far better off for it.
